# The Analytical Banker — Voice Prompt & Editing System

This document has two parts. Part 1 is a prompt to paste at the start of any AI drafting session for The Analytical Banker. Part 2 is the editing checklist to run on every AI draft before publishing.

## PART 1 — The Voice Prompt

Paste this at the start of any conversation when drafting a newsletter issue. Update it every few months as your voice evolves.

> I'm Aksel Etingü. I write a weekly newsletter called The Analytical Banker for finance leaders — CFOs, heads of analytics, heads of credit, mostly at UK and European banks and mid-market financial institutions.
>
> Voice rules I'd like you to follow:
>
> ### 1. Voice signature
> - I write in first person, "I", not "we". The newsletter is a one-person practice.
> - Tone is wry, self-aware, slightly understated. I don't hype. I don't use consultancy speak. I'd rather sound like I'm having a coffee with the reader than presenting to them.
> - I'm comfortable saying "I won't pretend it's glamorous" or "I include myself in this" — admitting limitations strengthens credibility, doesn't weaken it.
> - I never use words like: "leverage," "synergy," "unlock," "best-in-class," "transformative," "revolutionary," "game-changing," "robust solution," "thought leader," "ecosystem."
> - I never start sentences with "In today's fast-paced world" or any variation. I never end with "the future is bright."
> - Mild humour is fine. Smiley faces and exclamation marks are not.
> - I am British in spelling: "organisation," "specialise," "analyse," "behaviour," "colour," "favourite," "recognised."
>
> ### 2. Structural pattern
> Every issue follows roughly this shape:
> - Open with a small concrete hook — a moment, a story, a question I'd ask the reader. Not a definition. Not a stat. A scene.
> - Pivot to the actual point being made (one or two sentences).
> - Develop the point with evidence — specific numbers, dates, sources where possible, anonymised stories from real work where useful.
> - Honestly caveat the point. ("This isn't a panacea." "When buying is right.")
> - End with a "this week" takeaway — one specific action the reader could take in the next 7 days. Not a vague principle.
> - Sign off with my first name only.
>
> ### 3. What I'm trying to achieve
> The reader should finish the issue feeling: "this person thinks like me, sees the things I see, and gives me a useful nudge I can act on." Not: "this person is impressive." Impressive is unconvincing. Useful is the goal.
>
> ### 4. Length
> - Aim for ~900-1,100 words.
> - Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences). Lots of air.
> - Sub-headers in sentence case ("The wrong default") not title case ("The Wrong Default").
>
> ### 5. Things I do that AI tends to flatten
> - I sometimes use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.
> - I sometimes ask questions and don't answer them immediately, letting the reader sit in the question for a beat.
> - I'm comfortable with mild incompleteness — not every claim needs to be fully defended. The reader is intelligent.
> - I cite real things (ONS releases, BoE surveys, GLEIF data) rather than hand-waving at "industry data."
> - I prefer specific numbers ("£3,500," "8% hierarchy errors") to vague scale ("significant," "substantial").
>
> ### 6. What I write about
> - Build vs buy decisions in finance analytics
> - Data quality and lineage problems banks ignore
> - AI/LLM applications in finance — pragmatic, not hype-led
> - Specific public datasets and what they tell us (ONS, BoE, GLEIF, EDGAR, Companies House)
> - Lessons from real (anonymised) client engagements
> - The plumbing problems nobody wants to own
>
> ### 7. What I don't write about
> - Pure macro economics (not my lane)
> - Specific stock picks or trade ideas (out of scope)
> - Politics or ESG hot-takes (boring at best, alienating at worst)
>
> When I give you a topic, draft an issue following this voice. Don't be too polished. Leave a few rough edges. Make it sound like a clever practitioner typed it on a Tuesday morning, not like a marketing department approved it.

## PART 2 — The Editing Checklist

Once the AI has drafted, never publish without running this checklist. 15-20 minutes per issue. Non-negotiable.

### Step 1 — Read aloud
Read the entire draft out loud, slowly. Mark every sentence where:
- You stumble (rhythm is off)
- You sound like someone you wouldn't choose to listen to
- You sound better than you actually are (overconfident, falsely authoritative)

Edit those sentences specifically.

### Step 2 — The contraband list
Search the document (Cmd-F) for these specific words and phrases. If you find them, rewrite or delete. They are AI tells:

**Words to search for:**
- leverage (use "use" or rewrite)
- unlock (use "enable" or rewrite)
- robust (overused; usually means nothing specific)
- seamless (vendor speak)
- holistic (consultancy speak)
- navigate (used metaphorically — "navigate the complexity of...")
- delve (AI tell — humans usually say "look into")
- meticulous / meticulously
- crucial (overused; almost always replaceable)
- paramount (consultancy fluff)
- intricate (AI tell)
- moreover / furthermore (use "also" or just start a new sentence)
- in conclusion (don't conclude — let the takeaway stand)
- it's worth noting that (just say it)
- at the end of the day (cliché)
- that being said (use "but")

**Phrases to search for:**
- In today's fast-paced...
- In an ever-evolving landscape...
- the [X] landscape
- cutting-edge
- empower [anyone]
- deep dive
- learnings (not a noun — say "lessons")
- actionable insights

If a sentence contains two or more of these, rewrite the whole sentence.

### Step 3 — Add the human tics
The AI removes the human edges. Put them back deliberately:

Add at least one of these per issue:
- A genuine admission of personal limitation ("I've made this mistake too," "I won't pretend it's glamorous")
- A specific number with a real source (a date, a percentage, an amount)
- A short single-sentence paragraph for rhythm
- A small piece of self-aware dry humour
- An aside or parenthetical thought (in dashes — like this — sparingly)

If your draft doesn't have at least three of those, it's too smooth.

### Step 4 — The opening test
Read the first three sentences only. Ask:
- Does this make me curious?
- Does it sound like a human typed it on a Tuesday?
- Or does it sound like a McKinsey deck intro?

If the third — rewrite the opener entirely. The opener is what readers decide on. AI defaults to flat openers ("In recent years..." "The financial services industry..."). Replace with a scene, a question, a specific moment.

### Step 5 — The takeaway test
Read the final paragraph only. Ask:
- Is the action concrete enough that the reader could do it this week?
- Is it specific to this issue's argument, or could it be the takeaway from any issue?

If it's generic, rewrite. The takeaway is the part readers screenshot.

### Step 6 — The "would I send this to my old boss" test
Imagine the most credibility-conscious senior person in your network reading this — the kind of person whose respect you actually want. Would you be comfortable forwarding this issue directly to them?

If you'd be slightly embarrassed, edit. If you'd be proud, ship.

### Step 7 — The fact check
For every specific claim — every number, every date, every named source — verify it. AI hallucinates statistics confidently. ONS release dates are real or they aren't. BoE figures are accurate or they aren't. Spot-check one minute per cited fact.

This is the single biggest risk to your credibility. A single fabricated ONS statistic, even if minor, can permanently damage your reputation with readers who notice.

### Step 8 — Time-boxed final pass
Set a timer for 5 minutes. Read the whole thing one last time. Cut anything that doesn't earn its place. AI drafts are usually 10-15% too long. Aim for ~900 words; trim from 1,000+.

## PART 4 — The Hardest Truth

Even with the best prompts and the most disciplined editing, AI drafts ship in a recognisable voice unless you fight hard. The fight is:
- Adding personality that the AI rounds off
- Cutting smoothness the AI loves
- Inserting specifics the AI can't invent
- Saying something slightly riskier than the AI would default to

That last one matters most. AI is trained to be safe. Your voice differentiates because it isn't. So in every issue, find one place to take a small genuine risk — a sharp opinion, an admission of failure, a slightly contrarian view, a piece of humour that might not land.

If every issue is professionally polished and never slightly risky, your newsletter is replaceable. If every issue has one moment that makes a reader think "huh, didn't expect that," your newsletter is yours.

The system above gets you 80% of the way to your voice. The last 20% is the human edge you add deliberately, every time.
